Lectures 18–20 · Determinants, cofactors, and the determinant as volume

The determinant is one number squeezed out of a square matrix that answers a geometric question: how much does the matrix scale (signed) area or volume? det = 0 means the columns collapse to a lower dimension — the matrix is singular. ▶ watch on OCW
The big picture. For a 2×2 matrix the determinant is the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by its columns; for 3×3 it is the signed volume of the parallelepiped. Drag the sliders below and watch the area/volume number track the determinant exactly — and watch it hit 0 the moment the vectors line up (singular), and flip sign when you swap two columns (orientation reverses).

Strang closes Lecture 20 with a gem: the area of a triangle with corners (x₁,y₁), (x₂,y₂), (x₃,y₃) needs no base, no height, no square roots — just the coordinates, through a 3×3 determinant:

Area = ½ · det [[x₁, y₁, 1], [x₂, y₂, 1], [x₃, y₃, 1]]

3 · The properties Strang uses to define the determinant

Strang builds the determinant from a short list of rules. Everything else (the formula, the cofactor expansion, Cramer's rule) follows from these:

det I = 1The unit box (columns = standard basis) has volume 1.
row swap → sign flipExchanging two rows multiplies the determinant by −1 (orientation reverses). Two equal rows ⇒ det = 0.
linear in each rowScaling one row by t scales det by t; and det is additive in any single row (other rows fixed).
dependent rows → det = 0If the rows (equivalently columns) are linearly dependent, the box is flat: the matrix is singular.
det(AB) = det(A)·det(B)Volumes multiply under composition; hence det(A⁻¹) = 1/det(A).
det(Aᵀ) = det(A)Transpose leaves the determinant unchanged — so every row rule is also a column rule.

5 · The "big formula" — a sum over all n! permutations

Before cofactors, Strang derives the big formula straight from the three properties. Split each row into its coordinate pieces (linearity), throw away every term with a repeated column (those have a zero column ⇒ determinant 0), and the survivors are exactly the products that take one entry from each row and each column. There are n! survivors — one per permutation — and each carries a + or − sign equal to det P of that permutation (+ for an even number of row exchanges, − for odd).

det A = Σ(over all permutations α,β,…,ω of 1…n) ± a1α·a2β·a3γ … anω

Strang's count: 2×2 has 2 terms (+ad − bc); 3×3 has 3! = 6 terms (half plus, half minus); 4×4 has 4! = 24; in general n!. Pick a size and watch the survivors — the permutation, its parity, and the signed product — enumerate themselves:

Each surviving term is a1,σ(1)·a2,σ(2)… where σ runs through every permutation. The +/− is det Pσ: the identity order 1 2 3 is +, a single swap like 2 1 3 is −, two swaps like 2 3 1 back to +. Notice the 4×4 counter-diagonal permutation 4 3 2 1 comes out + (it takes two exchanges, not one) — Strang's warning that the "wrong diagonal" is only minus for 3×3.

6 · Strang's tridiagonal-of-ones example — a periodic determinant

Strang's favourite cofactor example: the n×n matrix that is all 1's on the main diagonal and the two neighbouring diagonals, 0 elsewhere (a tridiagonal matrix of ones). Cofactor expansion along the first row gives the beautiful recursion

Dn = Dn−1 − Dn−2  (the − comes from the off-diagonal 1, whose cofactor carries a minus sign).

7 · Cramer's rule and the inverse

For an invertible A, the solution of Ax = b has each coordinate as a ratio of determinants:

xi = det(Bi) / det(A), where Bi is A with its i-th column replaced by b. (This is exactly the formula used in LA.analyze.)

And the inverse is the transposed cofactor matrix (the adjugate) divided by the determinant:

A⁻¹ = (1 / det A) · Cᵀ   where C is the matrix of cofactors.

Worked math

2×2 via cofactors. Expanding [[a, b], [c, d]] along the first row: the cofactor of a is +d, the cofactor of b is −c, so

det = a·(+d) + b·(−c) = ad − bc.

Geometrically |ad − bc| is the parallelogram's area: base |v₁| times height (the component of v₂ perpendicular to v₁). Shearing v₂ along v₁ changes neither — matching "adding a multiple of one row to another leaves det unchanged."

3×3 via cofactors (general formula).

det = a₁₁(a₂₂a₃₃ − a₂₃a₃₂) − a₁₂(a₂₁a₃₃ − a₂₃a₃₁) + a₁₃(a₂₁a₃₂ − a₂₂a₃₁).

This is precisely the expression coded in LA.det3. Its absolute value is the parallelepiped's volume = base-area × perpendicular height; the sign records orientation (right- vs left-handed triple).

Worked cofactor expansion. Take

A = [[2, 1, 1], [1, 3, 2], [1, 0, 0]].

Expanding along the bottom row (two zeros make it cheap) — signs [+, −, +] on row 3:

det = 1·(+1)·det[[1,1],[3,2]] − 0 + 0
    = 1·(1·2 − 1·3) = 1·(2 − 3) = −1.

Check along the first row instead: 2·det[[3,2],[0,0]] − 1·det[[1,2],[1,0]] + 1·det[[1,3],[1,0]] = 2·0 − 1·(0 − 2) + 1·(0 − 3) = 0 + 2 − 3 = −1. Same answer, as the properties guarantee.

Cramer's-rule example. Solve

2x + y = 5,   1x + 3y = 6.   So A = [[2,1],[1,3]], b = (5, 6).

det A = 2·3 − 1·1 = 5. Replace columns with b:

B₁ = [[5,1],[6,3]] ⇒ det B₁ = 5·3 − 1·6 = 9,
B₂ = [[2,5],[1,6]] ⇒ det B₂ = 2·6 − 5·1 = 7.
x = det B₁ / det A = 9/5 = 1.8,   y = det B₂ / det A = 7/5 = 1.4.

Check: 2(1.8) + 1.4 = 5 ✓ and 1.8 + 3(1.4) = 6 ✓.
