Lecture 24 · Markov matrices, and a note on Fourier series

A Markov matrix has nonnegative entries and columns that sum to 1. Such a matrix always has eigenvalue λ = 1; every other eigenvalue has |λ| < 1. So repeated multiplication drives any starting state to a single steady state — the eigenvector for λ = 1. We close with the same idea applied to functions: Fourier coefficients are projections onto an orthonormal basis. ▶ watch on OCW
The example. We use Strang's classic matrix A = [[0.9, 0.2], [0.1, 0.8]]. Think of two cities: each year 10% of city 1's people move to city 2, and 20% of city 2's people move to city 1. The total population is conserved (columns sum to 1), and no matter how it starts, the populations settle to a fixed split — the steady state.

Intuition · why a steady state must exist

A Markov matrix moves "stuff" (people, probability, dollars) between states without creating or destroying it — that is exactly what columns summing to 1 guarantees. The total 1ᵀuₖ is conserved at every step. Because nothing leaks out and the entries are nonnegative, the state cannot blow up and cannot collapse to zero; it has to head somewhere and stay there. That somewhere is the steady state: the one distribution that A leaves unchanged, Au = u.

Why does it forget where it started? Write u₀ in the eigenvector basis, u₀ = c₁x₁ + c₂x₂ + …. Then uₖ = c₁λ₁ᵏx₁ + c₂λ₂ᵏx₂ + …. The λ = 1 term holds its size forever; every other term has |λ| < 1, so λᵏ → 0. The transient parts decay and only the steady-state direction survives. The second-largest |λ| sets the speed: closer to 1 means slower mixing (try the "Slow mixing" preset).

Worked math · the example A = [[0.9, 0.2], [0.1, 0.8]]

1 · Columns sum to 1 ⇒ λ = 1 is an eigenvalue. Let 1 = (1, 1, …, 1). "Columns of A sum to 1" means 1ᵀA = 1ᵀ, i.e. 1ᵀ(A − I) = 0. So the rows of A − I are dependent (they add to the zero row), which makes A − I singular: det(A − I) = 0. That is the statement that λ = 1 is an eigenvalue. (Equivalently: A and Aᵀ share eigenvalues, and Aᵀ1 = 1, so 1 is a left eigenvector for λ = 1.)

2 · Find both eigenvalues. For our matrix, trace = 0.9 + 0.8 = 1.7 and det = 0.9·0.8 − 0.2·0.1 = 0.72 − 0.02 = 0.70. The eigenvalues satisfy λ² − 1.7λ + 0.70 = 0, giving λ = 1 and λ = 0.7. Note λ₁ + λ₂ = trace and λ₁λ₂ = det. The second eigenvalue 0.7 < 1, so its contribution decays like 0.7ᵏ.

3 · The steady-state eigenvector (λ = 1). Solve (A − I)x = 0: A − I = [[−0.1, 0.2], [0.1, −0.2]]. Both rows give −x₁ + 2x₂ = 0, so x₁ = 2x₂ and x₁ = (2, 1) spans the line. Normalizing so the entries sum to the total population gives the split ⅔, ⅓. With u₀ = (0, 1000) (total 1000) the steady state is (666.7, 333.3) — and that is exactly where the path lands, independent of the start.

4 · The eigenvector for λ = 0.7. Solve (A − 0.7I)x = 0: [[0.2, 0.2],[0.1, 0.1]]x = 0 gives x = (1, −1). Its entries sum to 0 — it carries no total, only the imbalance that mixing erases. So uₖ = (steady state) + c·(0.7)ᵏ(1, −1), and the wobble dies out as 0.7ᵏ → 0.

Visual · why λ = 1, on Strang's invented 3×3 Markov matrix

In lecture Strang first writes down a "totally just invented" 3×3 Markov matrix A = [[.1, .2, .7], [.01, .99, 0], [.3, .3, .4]] to show why λ = 1 is forced. The trick: subtract the identity. Each column of A sums to 1, so each column of A − I sums to 1 − 1 = 0. A matrix whose columns add to the zero column is singular — and the combination that kills the rows is the all-ones vector 1ᵀ(A − I) = 0. So 1 = (1,1,1) is a left eigenvector, det(A − I) = 0, and λ = 1 is an eigenvalue. The plot makes the "columns sum to 0" fact literal: every red bar lands on zero.

A note on Fourier series · projection onto an orthonormal basis

The same projection idea that finds a steady state from eigenvectors also explains Fourier series. To project a vector v onto an orthonormal basis q₁, q₂, … you take inner products: v = (q₁ᵀv) q₁ + (q₂ᵀv) q₂ + … — each coefficient is just a dot product.

Functions work the same way, with the inner product ⟨f, g⟩ = ∫₀²π f(x)g(x) dx. The functions 1, cos x, sin x, cos 2x, sin 2x, … are orthogonal under this inner product (e.g. ∫₀²π cos x · sin x dx = 0). A Fourier series writes f(x) = a₀ + a₁cos x + b₁sin x + a₂cos 2x + …, and each coefficient is a projection: aₖ = ⟨f, cos kx⟩ / ⟨cos kx, cos kx⟩, bₖ = ⟨f, sin kx⟩ / ⟨sin kx, sin kx⟩ — the continuous twin of qₖᵀv.
