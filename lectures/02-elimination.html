Lecture 2 · Elimination with matrices

Strang's method: turn Ax = b into an upper-triangular system Ux = c by subtracting multiples of one row from another — then read the answer off by back-substitution. ▶ watch on OCW

What you're seeing

Each of the three equations is a plane (the row picture from Lecture 1). They all pass through the single solution point, drawn as a black dot. Elimination never moves that point: when we replace "row 2" by "row 2 − 3·row 1" we are subtracting one true equation from another, so the new equation is still satisfied by the same solution. Geometrically the plane swivels about the line where it met plane 1, but it keeps passing through the meeting point of all three. By the last step the system is triangular: the third plane has been tilted until its equation involves only z, the second only y, z, and the first all three — that staircase is exactly what makes back-substitution work.

The yellow boxes mark the pivots — the first nonzero entry in each row once we reach it. Here they are 1, 2, 5. Pivots are the numbers we divide by, so they must be nonzero; their product (1·2·5 = 10) equals det A, which is why a zero pivot signals a singular matrix.

Why a matrix on the left does row operations

Strang justifies the elimination matrices with one fact about multiplication. Last lecture: a matrix × column = a combination of the columns — his example, A·(3,4,5) is 3·col₁ + 4·col₂ + 5·col₃. Today he needs the mirror image: a row × matrix = a combination of the rows. His example is the row (1, 2, 7) times A, which equals 1·row₁ + 2·row₂ + 7·row₃. Click the toggle to see the same matrix read both ways — that is exactly why E₂₁'s second row (−3, 1, 0) means "take −3 of row 1 plus 1 of row 2."

Permutation matrices — and why rows go left, columns go right

Strang's "slight digression": the other elementary matrix, the one that exchanges two rows, is a permutation matrix P. The easy way to build it is to do the swap to the identity. On a 2×2 [[a,b],[c,d]], multiplying P = [[0,1],[1,0]] on the left swaps the rows to [[c,d],[a,b]]. To exchange columns instead, the same P must go on the right, giving [[b,a],[d,c]]. His rule: "to do row operations the matrix multiplies on the left; to do column operations it multiplies on the right."

The inverse — undoing one elimination step

Strang's closing "leg up on inverses." Take E = E₂₁ = [[1,0,0],[−3,1,0],[0,0,1]], which subtracts 3·row 1 from row 2. To undo it you add 3·row 1 back to row 2, so E⁻¹ = [[1,0,0],[3,1,0],[0,0,1]] — same matrix with the sign of the multiplier flipped. Their product is the identity: E⁻¹·E = I. Concretely, E turned b = (2,12,2) into (2,6,2); E⁻¹ turns it straight back.

Pivots, row exchanges, and breakdown

"No row exchange needed" means every pivot — the entry we divide by at each stage — came out nonzero on its own (here 1, then 2, then 5), so we could march straight down the diagonal. That is the lucky, typical case.

When elimination breaks down: if a pivot position holds a 0, we cannot divide by it. Two things can happen:

  • Temporary zero — fix with a permutation. If some lower row has a nonzero entry in that column, swap the two rows (multiply by a permutation matrix P) and continue. For example, [[0,2],[3,4]] has a 0 in the first pivot, but exchanging rows gives the nonzero pivot 3.
  • Fatal zero — singular matrix. If the whole column below is also 0, no exchange helps. A pivot is genuinely missing, the matrix is singular (det = 0, since det = product of pivots), and the system has either no solution or infinitely many — the singular cases from Lecture 1.

Strang's own "what if" tweaks to this matrix. He poked at the example to show where trouble would hide:

  • If the (2,2) entry 8 were a 6 instead, then after row 2 − 3·row 1 the second pivot would be 6 − 6 = 0 — a temporary zero, but row 3 has a nonzero entry below it, so a row exchange rescues us. "If this 8 had been a 6 … I would survive by a row exchange."
  • If the (3,3) entry 1 were a −4 instead, the third pivot becomes 5 + (−4) − 1 = 0 with nothing below to swap in — a fatal zero. "There wouldn't have been a third pivot. The matrix would not have been invertible." That is the one tweak Strang couldn't get out of. Use the slider below to dial that bottom-right entry and watch the third pivot collapse.
