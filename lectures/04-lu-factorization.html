Lecture 4 · Factorization A = LU

Elimination isn't just a way to solve Ax = b — it factors the matrix. The row operations live in a lower-triangular L, and the result lives in an upper-triangular U. The big idea: L is just a record of the elimination multipliers. ▶ watch on OCW
Warm-up (2×2). Take A = [[2, 1], [8, 7]]. One elimination step clears the 8: subtract 4× row 1 from row 2 (the multiplier is ℓ₂₁ = 4). That leaves U = [[2, 1], [0, 3]], and recording the multiplier gives L = [[1, 0], [4, 1]]. Check: L·U = A.

The geometry: L shears, U scales

A 2×2 view makes the factorization tangible. Using the warm-up A = [[2,1],[8,7]] = L·U with L = [[1,0],[4,1]], U = [[2,1],[0,3]], watch the unit square get transformed. Apply U first (an upper-triangular map — scaling plus a horizontal shear), then L (a lower-triangular map — a pure vertical shear). The composition lands exactly on A's image. Triangular pieces are simple motions; their product is the full transformation.

What you're seeing

The stepper. Elimination drives A to upper-triangular form U by subtracting multiples of higher rows from lower rows. The multiplier you use to kill the (i, j) entry — call it ℓᵢⱼ — is exactly the number that belongs in position (i, j) of L. You never recompute L; you just collect the multipliers as you go. That is why L is lower-triangular with 1's on the diagonal: the diagonal records "1× this row stays," and below it sit the subtractions.

Why it works. Each elimination step is left-multiplication by an elementary matrix Eᵢⱼ. Doing them all gives E … E A = U, so A = (E … E)⁻¹ U = L U. The miracle is that L = (E … E)⁻¹ assembles with no extra work: the inverses just put each multiplier back with a plus sign, in its own slot, with no cross-terms — provided we eliminate top-down and left-to-right.

The geometry. An upper-triangular U keeps the x-axis structured and scales/shears; a lower-triangular L with unit diagonal is a shear that leaves the first coordinate alone and pushes the second. Neither distorts area beyond its diagonal product, and det A = det L · det U = (1)·(product of pivots).

The math, done by hand

Eliminate A = [[2,1,0],[4,5,2],[2,5,5]] column by column. The first pivot is 2 (row 1).

Step 1 — clear column 1, row 2. Multiplier ℓ₂₁ = 4 / 2 = 2. Do R2 ← R2 − 2·R1:

R2:4 5 2− 2·(2 1 0) =0 3 2

Step 2 — clear column 1, row 3. Multiplier ℓ₃₁ = 2 / 2 = 1. Do R3 ← R3 − 1·R1:

R3:2 5 5− 1·(2 1 0) =0 4 5

Step 3 — clear column 2, row 3. The second pivot is now 3 (the updated row 2). Multiplier ℓ₃₂ = 4 / 3. Do R3 ← R3 − (4/3)·R2:

R3:0 4 5− (4/3)·(0 3 2) =0 0 7/3

The pivots are 2, 3, 7/3, so

U = [[2, 1, 0], [0, 3, 2], [0, 0, 7/3]]

Assemble L with 1's on the diagonal and each multiplier in its own slot (i, j):

L = [[1, 0, 0], [ℓ₂₁=2, 1, 0], [ℓ₃₁=1, ℓ₃₂=4/3, 1]]

Multiplying confirms L·U = A, and det A = 2 · 3 · 7/3 = 14 (= product of pivots, since det L = 1).

The real magic: why L (not E) is the clean one

The deepest point in the lecture is why we write A = L U instead of E A = U. Strang's example: suppose E₂₁ subtracts 2×row 1 from row 2, and E₃₂ subtracts 5×row 2 from row 3 (and E₃₁ = I). Multiply the E's together and an ugly extra entry appears — a spurious 10 in position (3,1) — because "two of row 1 got removed from row 2, and then five of that got removed from row 3, so altogether ten of row 1 got thrown into row 3." Press the button to watch the 10 appear in E, then vanish in L.

Reverse order (E₃₂E₂₁)⁻¹ = E₂₁⁻¹E₃₂⁻¹ is what makes the multipliers drop straight into place.
Row exchanges. This all assumed no zero pivots, so we never had to swap rows. If a pivot comes out zero (or you swap for numerical stability), record the swaps in a permutation matrix P and you get P·A = L·U instead. Every square matrix has such a factorization.

How expensive is elimination? About ⅓ n³

"Can we think together how expensive is elimination?" The first step changes essentially every entry of an n×n matrix — cost ≈ n². The next step works on the remaining (n−1)×(n−1) block — cost ≈ (n−1)² — and so on down to 1². The total is n² + (n−1)² + ⋯ + 2² + 1² ≈ ⅓ n³ The ⅓ is exactly the calculus fact ∫₀ⁿ x² dx = ⅓ n³: "somehow that one third takes account of the fact that the numbers are getting smaller." The bars below are the per-step costs k²; their total area fills about one third of the full n³ box.

And the right-hand side b? "It's just one column," carried through elimination and back substitution — only n² operations. So you pay the one-time ⅓n³ price to factor A = LU, then solve each new b cheaply at n². (Determinants by cofactors would cost n! — far worse.)

Permutations: the six 3×3 matrices, and P⁻¹ = Pᵀ

When a zero shows up in a pivot position you swap rows, and the swaps are recorded in a permutation matrix P — "all the ways I can take the identity matrix and rearrange its rows." For 3×3 there are exactly 3! = 6 of them (the identity, three single-swaps, and two 3-cycles). They form a group: products and inverses of permutations are again permutations, and the "curious fact" is that P⁻¹ = Pᵀ. Click any matrix to verify P·Pᵀ = I.

For 4×4 there are 4! = 24, and in general n!. With permutations in hand, every square matrix factors as P·A = L·U.
