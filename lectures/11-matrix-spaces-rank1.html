Lecture 11 · Matrix spaces; rank 1

A rank-1 matrix is an outer product A = u·vᵀ — every column is a multiple of u, so the column space is a single line. And the matrices themselves form vector spaces: 3×3 matrices live in a 9-dimensional space. ▶ watch on OCW

Part 1 — Rank-1 matrices: A = u vᵀ

The idea. Pick a column vector u and a row vector vᵀ. Their outer product A = u vᵀ is a full matrix, but its rank is 1: every column of A is a scalar multiple of u. Part 2 — The space of all 3×3 matrices

The idea. All 3×3 matrices can be added and scaled and stay 3×3, so they form a vector space — just like vectors do. Its dimension is 9 (the nine entries are nine free numbers). Several important sets of matrices are subspaces: symmetric (6), upper-triangular (6), and diagonal (3). Toggle one below to light up its free entries and see the dimension count.

Worked math — why A = u vᵀ has rank 1

Write u = (u₁,…,uₘ)ᵀ and v = (v₁,…,vₙ)ᵀ. The outer product is the m×n matrix 

A = u vᵀ =  ⎡ u₁v₁  u₁v₂  …  u₁vₙ ⎤
            ⎢ u₂v₁  u₂v₂  …  u₂vₙ ⎥
            ⎣ uₘv₁  uₘv₂  …  uₘvₙ ⎦

Column j is (u₁vⱼ, u₂vⱼ, …, uₘvⱼ)ᵀ = vⱼ · u. So every column is the same vector u multiplied by a scalar vⱼ. The set of all such columns is exactly { t·u : t ∈ ℝ } — the line through the origin in the direction of u.

Therefore the column space is 1-dimensional (assuming u ≠ 0 and v ≠ 0), and rank A = dim C(A) = 1. By the same argument applied to rows, every row is a multiple of vᵀ, so the row space is also the single line through v — rank from rows equals rank from columns, both 1. (Strang's fact: row rank = column rank.) Conversely, every rank-1 matrix can be written as some u vᵀ: pick any nonzero column for u, and the multipliers that produce the other columns become the entries of vᵀ.

Worked math — dimensions of the matrix subspaces

A standard basis for the space M of all 3×3 matrices is the nine matrices E₍ᵢⱼ₎ with a single 1 in position (i,j) and zeros elsewhere. They are independent and span M, so dim M = 9. The subspaces count their own free entries:

  • Symmetric (Aᵀ = A): 3 diagonal entries + 3 strictly-upper entries that determine their mirrors ⇒ dim = 3 + 3 = 6.
  • Upper-triangular (zeros below the diagonal): the 6 entries on and above the diagonal are free ⇒ dim = 6.
  • Diagonal: only the 3 diagonal entries are free ⇒ dim = 3.

Intersection. A matrix that is both symmetric and upper-triangular must have all below-diagonal entries 0 (upper-triangular) and equal to the mirror above-diagonal entries (symmetric) — forcing the above-diagonal entries to 0 too. What's left is diagonal: Symmetric ∩ Upper = Diagonal, of dimension 3.

The dimension formula. For subspaces S and U of the same space, dim(S+U) = dim S + dim U − dim(S∩U). Here dim(Sym + Upper) = 6 + 6 − 3 = 9, which equals dim M — so the symmetric and upper-triangular matrices together span every 3×3 matrix. (Indeed any A splits as a symmetric part ½(A+Aᵀ) plus an "upper-correction," covering all of M.)

Worked math — Strang's rank-1 example [1 4 5; 2 8 10]

Strang builds a concrete rank-1 matrix on the board. Start with the first row (1 4 5) and ask: what second row keeps the rank at 1? The answer is any multiple of the first — he picks (2 8 10) = 2·(1 4 5): 

A = ⎡ 1  4   5 ⎤   =  ⎡ 1 ⎤
    ⎣ 2  8  10 ⎦      ⎣ 2 ⎦ · ( 1  4  5 )

The second row is a multiple of the first, so the rows are not independent: a basis for the row space is just (1 4 5) — dim(row space) = 1. The columns are all multiples of (1, 2)ᵀ, so dim(column space) = 1 too. Both equal the rank, r = 1 (Strang: column rank = row rank). The clean way to see it: A = u·vᵀ with the pivot column u = (1, 2)ᵀ times the row vᵀ = (1 4 5) — a 2×1 times a 1×3 giving a 2×3 matrix. Every rank-1 matrix factors this way; rank-1 matrices are the building blocks for all matrices (a rank-r matrix is a sum of r of them).

Part 3 — Small-world graphs: six degrees of separation

The idea. A graph here is not a curve — it's a set of nodes (people) joined by edges (friendships). Strang asks: in the friendship graph of an entire country (≈ 260 million nodes), how many steps does it take to get from anybody to anybody? Surprisingly few. His own distance to President Clinton is just 2 ("I went to college with somebody who knows Clinton"). The folklore answer for the whole world is six degrees of separation — and a handful of long-range shortcuts collapse the typical distance dramatically. A few long-range shortcuts (the dashed chords) act like Strang's "I know a Senator who knows Clinton" — they slash the diameter (the largest shortest-path distance between any two nodes). This is exactly why the World Wide Web — nodes = sites, edges = links — is also a small world.

Why linear algebra? A graph with n nodes and m edges is captured by an m×n incidence matrix (Strang's 5-node, 6-edge example gives a 5×6 matrix that "tells us everything about that graph"). Powers of the adjacency matrix count walks of a given length, so the smallest power whose entry is nonzero is the distance — turning "six degrees of separation" into a matrix question. Strang carries this into Lecture 12.
