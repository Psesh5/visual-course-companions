Lectures 25 & 27 · Symmetric matrices & positive definiteness

A symmetric matrix A = Aᵀ has real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors. It is positive definite when xᵀAx > 0 for every x ≠ 0 — the quadratic form is a bowl with a true minimum at the origin. This is the matrix version of the second-derivative test. ▶ watch on OCW

For a symmetric matrix these four tests are equivalent — all pass together or all fail together. Any one of them certifies positive definiteness.

What you're seeing

The surface (left). Every symmetric 2×2 matrix defines a quadratic form f(x,y) = xᵀA x = a·x² + 2b·xy + c·y². When A is positive definite the surface is a bowl opening upward with its single lowest point at the origin: f > 0 in every direction. Make A indefinite (one positive, one negative eigenvalue) and the bowl becomes a saddle — going uphill along one eigenvector and downhill along the other, so the origin is no longer a minimum. A semidefinite matrix gives a flat trough: f = 0 along a whole line.

The ellipse (right). Slice the bowl at height f = 1. The cut is the level set xᵀA x = 1, which for a positive-definite A is an ellipse. Its axes point exactly along the eigenvectors of A, and the half-length along the eigenvector with eigenvalue λ is 1/√λ. Big eigenvalue ⇒ steep direction ⇒ short axis; small eigenvalue ⇒ shallow direction ⇒ long axis. When A is not positive definite the level set f = 1 is no longer a closed ellipse (it opens into a hyperbola, or a pair of lines), so the ellipse panel reports that.

Why positive definite means "minimum"

For a function of several variables, the second-derivative test uses the matrix of second partials (the Hessian H). A critical point is a true minimum exactly when H is positive definite. For our quadratic f = a·x² + 2b·xy + c·y² the Hessian is the constant matrix H = [[2a, 2b],[2b, 2c]] = 2A, so "positive definite" and "the bowl has a minimum at the origin" are the same statement. The single-variable rule f''>0 becomes the matrix rule A ≻ 0.

The spectral theorem · A = QΛQᵀ (orthogonal eigenvectors)

Strang's "one of the famous theorems of linear algebra": every symmetric matrix factors as A = QΛQᵀ, where Q holds orthonormal eigenvectors in its columns (so Q⁻¹ = Qᵀ) and Λ is the diagonal of real eigenvalues. He also called it the principal axis theorem — the eigenvectors are the principal axes. Below, the left panel shows the two orthonormal eigenvectors q₁, q₂ sitting at a perfect right angle, with the f=1 ellipse they generate. The slider rotates the picture by Qᵀ: in those coordinates the ellipse lines up with the axes and A becomes the diagonal matrix Λ — coupling between x and y disappears, exactly Strang's "if I look at the right axis, it becomes diagonal."

Why it's beautiful (Strang). Transpose QΛQᵀ and you get QΛQᵀ back — the form is automatically symmetric. Multiplying it out column-times-row breaks every symmetric matrix into a sum of perpendicular projection matrices: A = λ₁ q₁q₁ᵀ + λ₂ q₂q₂ᵀ. Each q q ᵀ is a rank-1 projection; the eigenvalues are the weights.

Signs of pivots = signs of eigenvalues. A separate symmetric-matrix fact Strang stresses: you never need to compute eigenvalues to count how many are positive. The number of positive pivots equals the number of positive eigenvalues (he imagines a 50×50 matrix: 28 positive pivots ⇒ exactly 28 positive eigenvalues). Pivots are cheap and stable; eigenvalues are expensive. Both have the same signs, and their products both equal det A. Watch the pivots and eigenvalues rows in the live test table above move together as you edit A — they flip sign at the same moment.

Worked math · A = [[2, 1],[1, 2]]

1 · Eigenvalues. Solve det(A − λI) = 0: (2 − λ)² − 1 = 0 ⇒ (2 − λ)² = 1 ⇒ 2 − λ = ±1 ⇒ λ = 1, 3. Both eigenvalues are real (forced by symmetry) and both are positive, so A is positive definite. The eigenvectors are (1, 1)/√2 for λ = 3 and (1, −1)/√2 for λ = 1 — and they are orthogonal, another guarantee of symmetry.

2 · Leading-minor (determinant) test. Check the upper-left determinants: D₁ = 2 > 0 and D₂ = det A = 2·2 − 1·1 = 3 > 0. All leading minors positive ⇒ PD. Equivalently the pivots from elimination are 2 and D₂/D₁ = 3/2, both positive.

3 · The quadratic form is a sum of squares. Completing the square, f = 2x² + 2xy + 2y² = 2(x + ½y)² + (3/2)y². Each coefficient (2 and 3/2 — the pivots!) is positive, so f > 0 for every (x,y) ≠ (0,0). That is xᵀA x > 0 directly.

4 · Why eigenvectors are the ellipse axes. Diagonalize A = QΛQᵀ with Q orthogonal. In the rotated coordinates u = Qᵀx the form becomes f = λ₁u₁² + λ₂u₂², and f = 1 is λ₁u₁² + λ₂u₂² = 1 — an ellipse with half-axes 1/√λ₁ and 1/√λ₂ along the u-axes, i.e. along the eigenvectors of A. For our matrix the half-axes are 1/√3 ≈ 0.577 (along (1,1)) and 1/√1 = 1 (along (1,−1)).

Putting it together. Eigenvalues > 0 ⇔ pivots > 0 ⇔ leading minors > 0 ⇔ xᵀA x > 0 ⇔ the quadratic is a bowl with a minimum. Positive definiteness is the multivariable second-derivative test, written in the language of matrices.
