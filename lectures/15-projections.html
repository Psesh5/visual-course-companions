Lecture 15 · Projections onto subspaces

How do you find the closest point in a subspace to a vector b? Drop a perpendicular. The foot of that perpendicular is the projection p, and the leftover e = b − p is the error, always orthogonal to the subspace. This is the geometry behind least squares. ▶ watch on OCW
Two pictures. First we project onto a line (a 1-D subspace, span of one vector a). Then onto a plane (the column space of a 3×2 matrix A). In both cases p is the shadow of b and e meets the subspace at a right angle. Drag the sliders for b and watch a·e stay zero.

Figure 1 · Projection onto a line

Line direction a = (2, 1, 1). Move the tip of b. The blue arrow is b, the green arrow on the line is the projection p = (aᵀb / aᵀa) a, and the orange arrow is the error e = b − p. The error is perpendicular to the line: a·e = 0.
Strang's two sanity checks. Double b and the projection doubles too (x̂ is linear in b). But double the direction a — or flip its sign — and p doesn't move at all: the extra factor of a upstairs is cancelled by the extra aᵀa downstairs, because the line hasn't changed. The matrix below confirms the line's projector is rank-1, symmetric, and idempotent, just like the plane's.

Figure 2 · Projection onto a plane (a column space)

The plane is the column space of 101011 — i.e. the span of a₁=(1,0,1) and a₂=(0,1,1). We project b with the full least-squares formula p = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ b. The green dot is the foot of the perpendicular inside the plane; the orange arrow e = b − p rises perpendicular to the whole plane, so Aᵀe = 0.

Figure 3 · Strang's example — the best straight line

Strang ends the lecture by motivating projection: "fitting by a line." He takes three data points at times t = 1, 2, 3 with values b = 1, 2, 2 — i.e. (1, 1), (2, 2), (3, 2) — and asks for the best line b = C + Dt. No line hits all three, so the three equations C + D = 1, C + 2D = 2, C + 3D = 2 have no solution. In matrix form Ax = b with 111123, x = (C, D), b = (1, 2, 2). Projection picks the x̂ = (Ĉ, D̂) that minimizes the total squared error — exactly the same p = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ b machinery as Figure 2, now in "data space."

The dots are the data; the blue line is the best fit b = Ĉ + D̂t. The green dots on the line are the fitted values p = Ax̂ (the projection of b into the column space of A), and the orange stems are the errors e = b − p. Least squares minimizes ‖e‖², and the normal equations AᵀAx̂ = Aᵀb force Aᵀe = 0 — the errors sum to zero and are uncorrelated with t. Strang finishes Lecture 15 here and solves the numbers in Lecture 16.

The intuition

A projection is a shadow. Shine a light straight down onto the subspace; the shadow of b is the projection p. Of all the points in the subspace, p is the one closest to b — and the reason is the right angle. The leftover vector e = b − p (the part of b that the subspace can't reach) points straight out of the subspace. Any other point would be farther because you'd add distance along the subspace to the same perpendicular gap (Pythagoras).

Why we care. Real data rarely lands exactly in the column space of A, so Ax = b has no solution. Projection gives the next best thing: the closest reachable point p = Ax̂. That x̂ is the least-squares solution — it minimizes ‖b − Ax‖. Lectures 16 (least squares) and 17 (orthogonal matrices) build directly on this picture.

The projection matrix. The map b → p is linear, so it is a matrix P with p = Pb. Two facts make geometric sense and are shown live below each figure: P is symmetric (P = Pᵀ) and idempotent (P² = P). Idempotent because projecting a shadow again does nothing — it's already in the subspace. The matrix I − P projects onto the orthogonal complement and sends b to the error e.

The math, worked out

1. Projection onto a line. The projection of b onto the line through a is some multiple p = x̂a. The defining condition is that the error is perpendicular to the line:

a ᵀ(b − x̂a) = 0 ⇒ aᵀb − x̂(aᵀa) = 0 ⇒ x̂ = aᵀb / aᵀa
p = x̂a = (aᵀb / aᵀa) a
P = a aᵀ / aᵀa (a rank-1 symmetric matrix, since p = Pb)

The factor aᵀb/aᵀa is how much of a you need. Note aaᵀ is a 3×3 matrix while aᵀa is a scalar — order matters.

2. Projection onto a column space (the plane). Now p must be a combination of the columns of A, so p = Ax̂ for some vector x̂. The error e = b − Ax̂ must be perpendicular to every column, i.e. orthogonal to the whole column space. "Perpendicular to all columns" is exactly Aᵀe = 0 — the normal equations:

Aᵀ(b − Ax̂) = 0 ⇒ AᵀA x̂ = Aᵀb ⇒ x̂ = (AᵀA)⁻¹ Aᵀb
p = A x̂ = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ b ⇒ P = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ

This is the same formula as the line, generalized: the scalar 1/aᵀa becomes the matrix (AᵀA)⁻¹, and aᵀb becomes Aᵀb. (AᵀA is invertible exactly when the columns of A are independent.)

3. Why e ⟂ column space. The point is that e lands in the left null space of A. The column space and left null space are orthogonal complements in ℝⁿ (Lecture 14), so asking Aᵀe = 0 is identical to asking that e be perpendicular to the subspace. Choosing x̂ to make this true is what minimizes the distance.

4. The two properties. With P = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ:

Pᵀ = (A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ)ᵀ = A((AᵀA)⁻¹)ᵀAᵀ = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ = P (symmetric)
P² = A(AᵀA)⁻¹(AᵀA)(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ = A(AᵀA)⁻¹Aᵀ = P (idempotent)

(AᵀA)⁻¹ is symmetric because AᵀA is. The interior (AᵀA)⁻¹(AᵀA) = I collapses in P². Both identities are checked numerically in the readout boxes above.
