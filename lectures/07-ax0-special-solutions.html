Lecture 7 · Solving Ax = 0 — pivot variables & special solutions

Elimination on A never changes its nullspace. Reduce to R (reduced row echelon form), read off the pivot and free columns, and each free variable hands you one special solution. Those special solutions span the whole nullspace. ▶ watch on OCW
The picture: here A is 2×3, so x lives in R³ and the nullspace is something you can see. Use the toggle to switch the second row. With independent rows (rank 2) the nullspace is a line; make the rows dependent (rank 1) and the nullspace opens up into a whole plane. Rotate the 3-D plot to feel it.

What you're seeing

The 3-D plot. Every point x = (x₁, x₂, x₃) that solves Ax = 0 is drawn. For rank 2 the two rows are independent constraints, leaving one free direction — the nullspace is a line through the origin. For rank 1 the second row is just a multiple of the first, so there is really only one constraint and two free directions — the nullspace is a plane through the origin (shaded). The colored arrows are the special solutions; they are a basis for the nullspace, so the line/plane is exactly their span.

The right panel. Elimination takes A → R. The columns holding a leading 1 are pivot columns (blue); the rest are free columns (orange). The count of free columns is the dimension of the nullspace, and it equals the number of arrows you see. The check at the bottom multiplies A by each special solution and confirms you get 0.

Why elimination preserves the nullspace

Row operations are: swap two equations, scale an equation, or add a multiple of one equation to another. None of these change the set of x that make every equation 0 — if each row dots to 0 against x, then so does any combination of rows. So N(A) = N(R). We solve the easy system Rx = 0 instead, and it has the same answers.

Worked math

Strang's actual example — the 3×4 matrix

The 2×3 cases above fit in R³ so we can see the nullspace. But the matrix Strang works in lecture 7 is bigger — 3 rows, 4 columns — so its nullspace lives in R⁴ and can't be drawn. Instead we follow his exact algorithm on the board: eliminate A → U (echelon/"staircase" form), then keep going to R (reduced row echelon), and read the two special solutions straight off the I / F blocks.

His matrix. A = [[1, 2, 2, 2], [2, 4, 6, 8], [3, 6, 8, 10]]. He points out two dependencies before touching it: column 2 = 2·column 1 (so column 2 is not independent), and row 3 = row 1 + row 2 (so row 3 is not independent). "All that should come out of elimination."

Step through elimination:

Reading the nullspace off R = [ I F ]

Once the pivot columns are cleaned to a leading 1 with zeros above and below, the pivot columns hold an identity block I and the free columns hold a block Strang calls F. The matrix whose columns are the special solutions is the nullspace matrix N = [ −F ; I ] — put the identity in the free rows, and −F drops into the pivot rows. Multiplying R·N gives the zero matrix, which is just "all special solutions at once."
