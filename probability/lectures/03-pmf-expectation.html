PMFs and expectation · the center of mass of a random variable

A discrete random variable X is described by its probability mass function p(k) = P(X = k) — how much probability sits on each possible value. The expectation E[X] = Σ k·p(k) is the weighted average of those values: the single number where all that mass would balance. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)
The picture: each blue bar is the probability p(k) that X lands on the value k — the bar heights are an honest pile of mass that sums to 1. The red ▲ fulcrum sits at the mean μ = E[X]: think of the bars as weights on a seesaw, and the triangle is the one pivot where the whole thing balances. The pale band is μ ± σ, one standard deviation either side, so you can see how spread out the distribution is. Pick a family, drag the parameters, and watch the mass — and the balance point — move.

What a PMF is — and the two rules it must obey

A discrete random variable takes values in some list — 0, 1, 2, … — and the PMF just records the probability of each: p(k) = P(X = k). It is the most complete description there is; once you know p, you know everything probabilistic about X. Only two rules apply, and both are visible in the bar chart above. First, every bar is non-negative, p(k) ≥ 0 — you cannot have a negative amount of probability. Second, the bars sum to one, Σ p(k) = 1 — all the probability has to go somewhere. The status box re-checks that second rule live by adding the bar heights; it always lands on 1.000.

Expectation is a weighted average — and the balance point

The expectation is defined as E[X] = Σ k·p(k): walk over every value k, weight it by how likely it is, and add. That is exactly a weighted average of the possible values, where the weights are the probabilities. If the probabilities were all equal it would be the ordinary average; because they are not, values with more mass pull E[X] toward them.

The mechanical picture is the one Tsitsiklis keeps coming back to: imagine the PMF as little weights placed on a ruler, the weight at position k being p(k). The expectation is the center of mass — the single point where you could put a fulcrum and have the ruler balance. That is the red ▲ in the plot. Notice it need not sit on a bar, and for a skewed distribution it gets dragged toward the long tail. Expectation is where the probability mass balances.

A worked sum — the fair die, by hand

Take the fair die. Every face is equally likely, so p(k) = 1/6 for k = 1,…,6. Apply the definition term by term: 

E[X] = Σ k·p(k)
     = 1·(1/6) + 2·(1/6) + 3·(1/6) + 4·(1/6) + 5·(1/6) + 6·(1/6)
     = (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) / 6
     = 21 / 6  =  3.5

The mean is 3.5 — a value the die can never actually show. That is the point of "expected" value: it is the long-run average of many rolls, not a prediction of any single roll. Roll the die ten thousand times, average the faces, and you land near 3.5; the fulcrum sits exactly at the middle of the symmetric mass. For the binomial there is a slicker route: X is a sum of n independent coin-flips, each worth p on average, so E[X] = np — no big sum required. For Binomial(3, ½) that is 3·½ = 1.5, and the direct sum 0·⅛ + 1·⅜ + 2·⅜ + 3·⅛ = 12/8 = 1.5 agrees. The status box runs exactly this check for whatever parameters you choose: the closed form and the brute-force Σ k·p(k) always match.

Variance — how wide is the pile?

Expectation says where the mass sits; variance says how far it typically spreads from there. The definition is itself an expectation — of the squared distance from the mean: Var(X) = E[(X − μ)²] = Σ (k − μ)²·p(k). Squaring keeps over- and under-shoots from cancelling and punishes far-out values. Its square root σ = √Var is the standard deviation, back in the original units, and it is the half-width of the pale band drawn around the fulcrum. Each family has a tidy closed form: Binomial has Var = np(1−p), the Poisson has the remarkable Var = λ = E[X] (mean and variance coincide), and the Geometric has Var = (1−p)/p². Drag p on the binomial toward 0 or 1 and watch the band collapse — a near-certain coin barely varies; the spread is widest at the fair p = ½.

The linearity teaser

The reason E[X] = np dropped out so cheaply is the single most useful fact about expectation: it is linear. For any random variables, E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y] — no independence needed, no shared distribution required — and E[a·X + b] = a·E[X] + b. A binomial is the sum X = X₁ + ⋯ + Xₙ of n Bernoulli(p) indicators, each with mean p, so its mean is just n copies of p added up. Variance is not linear in general — it only adds when the pieces are independent, which is exactly why the binomial's variance is n·p(1−p). That split — expectation always adds, variance adds only under independence — is the hinge the next several lectures turn on.
