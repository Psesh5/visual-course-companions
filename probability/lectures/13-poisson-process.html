The Poisson process · arrivals in continuous time

Points arrive at random along a timeline at an average rate λ per unit time. Three facts run the whole story: the count of arrivals in a window of length T is Poisson(λT); the gaps between consecutive arrivals are Exponential(λ) with mean 1/λ; and the exponential is memoryless — the wait for the next arrival forgets how long you have already waited. This is the continuous-time limit of the Bernoulli process (Lecture 12). ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

From the Bernoulli process to the Poisson process

In the Bernoulli process time comes in discrete slots, and each slot independently contains an arrival with probability p. The number of arrivals in n slots is Binomial(n, p), and the wait between arrivals is Geometric(p). Now slice continuous time into more and more tiny slots: let the slot width go to zero and the per-slot probability p shrink with it, holding the rate fixed at n·p = λT arrivals per window. In that limit two classic convergences fire at once:

  • Binomial → Poisson. Binomial(n, λT/n) → Poisson(λT). The count of arrivals in a window of length T is P(N = k) = e−λT(λT)k/k!.
  • Geometric → Exponential. The discrete waiting time Geometric(p) rescales to the continuous Exponential(λ), with density λe−λt and mean 1/λ.

So the Poisson process is nothing more exotic than the Bernoulli process with the clock turned continuous. Everything you knew about coin-flips-per-slot carries over to arrivals-per-second.

Fact 1 — the count is Poisson(λT)

Fix a window of length T. The number of arrivals N inside it is Poisson(λT), so its mean and its variance are the same number: E[N] = λT and Var(N) = λT. That equality is the Poisson signature — if someone hands you count data whose variance badly exceeds its mean, the arrivals are not a clean Poisson process (they are clustered, or "over-dispersed"). With the moderate preset, λ = 1.5, T = 10, you expect λT = 15 arrivals, give or take √15 ≈ 3.9. Switch the bottom plot to the count view and watch the histogram of counts over thousands of runs settle onto the black Poisson(15) stems.

Fact 2 — the gaps are Exponential(λ)

Equivalently, you can build the process gap-by-gap: the time until the first arrival, and every inter-arrival time after it, is an independent Exponential(λ) draw with density λe−λt and mean 1/λ. That is exactly how the top plot is generated — we accumulate PR.exponential(λ) gaps until we run off the end of the window. At λ = 1.5 the typical gap is 1/λ ≈ 0.67 units, but the exponential is heavily right-skewed: many short gaps, occasional long ones. Those long gaps next to short ones are precisely why the timeline looks clumpy rather than tidy. Flip the bottom plot to the gap view: the histogram of gaps from one very long run lies right under the smooth λe−λt curve.

Fact 3 — the exponential is memoryless

The defining property of the exponential gap is that it forgets the past: 

P(T > s + t | T > s) = P(T > t)        for all s, t ≥ 0

  proof:  P(T > s+t | T > s) = P(T > s+t) / P(T > s)
                              = e^(−λ(s+t)) / e^(−λs)
                              = e^(−λt) = P(T > t)

In words: if a bus arrives as a Poisson process and you have already waited s minutes with no bus, your remaining wait has the same distribution as if you had just walked up — fresh Exponential(λ), mean 1/λ. The process has no memory and no momentum; "it's overdue, so it must come soon" is a fallacy here. This is also why you can split any window anywhere and the pieces stay independent Poisson processes: the past tells you nothing about the future.

Where this shows up

The Poisson process is the default model for events that happen one at a time, independently, at a steady average rate: phone calls reaching a call center, photons hitting a detector, clicks on a Geiger counter from radioactive decay, requests landing on a web server, and trade ticks printing on an exchange. Whenever you can argue "rare, independent, constant-rate," reach for Poisson counts and exponential gaps — and remember the one-line sanity check that the variance of the count should equal its mean.
