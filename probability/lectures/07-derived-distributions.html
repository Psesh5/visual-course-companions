Derived distributions · how a density transforms

You know the distribution of X. You build a new random variable Y = g(X) — square it, exponentiate it, scale it. What is the density of Y? The answer is the change-of-variables formula: where g is steep the density gets stretched thin, where g is flat probability piles up. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

The method — first the CDF, then differentiate

There is exactly one safe recipe, and it never involves "just plug into a formula" first. To find the distribution of Y = g(X) you first chase down its CDF: FY(y) = P(Y ≤ y) = P(g(X) ≤ y). You rewrite the event {g(X) ≤ y} as an event about X — a set of x-values — and read off its probability from the distribution you already have. Then, and only then, you differentiate: fY(y) = d/dy FY(y). Tsitsiklis is insistent on the order: get the CDF honestly, then take the derivative. Skipping to a density formula is how people drop a factor or a whole branch.

The change-of-variables formula

When g is monotonic and smooth, doing that CDF calculation once in general gives a clean shortcut. If g is increasing, g(X) ≤ y is the same event as X ≤ g⁻¹(y), so FY(y) = FX(g⁻¹(y)), and the chain rule hands you 

f_Y(y) = f_X( g⁻¹(y) ) · | d/dy g⁻¹(y) |          ← the Jacobian |dx/dy|

The absolute value is there so a decreasing g gives the same positive density. The whole content lives in that Jacobian |dx/dy| = 1 / |g′(x)|. Read it as a stretch factor: where g is steep (|g′| large) a wide band of x gets squeezed into a thin band of y, so the density is divided down — stretched thin. Where g is flat (|g′| small) a sliver of x spreads across a wide band of y, and probability piles up. The density of Y is just the density of X reweighted by how much g locally stretches the axis.

The non-monotonic case — add the branches

Press Normal → X². Now g(x) = x² is not monotonic: both x = +√y and x = −√y land on the same y. The CDF method handles this automatically — {X² ≤ y} = {−√y ≤ X ≤ √y} — and differentiating picks up both ends: 

F_Y(y) = F_X(√y) − F_X(−√y)
f_Y(y) = [ f_X(√y) + f_X(−√y) ] · 1/(2√y) ,   y > 0

You sum one Jacobian-weighted term per branch of g⁻¹. For X ~ Uniform[0,1] only the +√y branch is in support, so fY(y) = 1/(2√y) on (0,1) — and notice it explodes as y → 0. That spike is the "flat g" intuition made literal: near x = 0 the parabola x² is nearly horizontal, so a fat clump of x near 0 gets crushed into a tiny sliver of y near 0, and the density there blows up. The histogram on the right shows the same pile-up; the analytic curve simply runs off the top of the frame.

The lognormal — why multiplicative noise is skewed

Press Normal → eˣ. Here g is monotonic, g⁻¹(y) = ln y, |dx/dy| = 1/y, so fY(y) = fX(ln y) / y = 1/(y√(2π)) · e−(ln y)²/2 for y > 0. This is the lognormal: a symmetric bell in x = ln y becomes a right-skewed hump in y, with a long tail to the right and a hard floor at 0. The mechanism is the 1/y Jacobian — the exponential is flat for small y (piling density up near 0) and steep for large y (thinning it into the tail). This is exactly why prices are modeled as lognormal while log-returns are modeled as normal: returns compound multiplicatively, and a symmetric distribution of log-returns produces an asymmetric, never-negative distribution of prices.

The linear case is the sanity check

For Y = aX + b the Jacobian is the constant 1/|a|: fY(y) = fX((y−b)/a) / |a|. Scaling by a just widens (or, for |a| < 1, narrows) the density and divides the height to keep the area at 1; adding b slides it. Feed it a normal and you get a normal back: N(0,1) → N(b, a²). Drag the a and b sliders and watch a stretch in x show up as a proportional thinning of the curve — the cleanest possible view of "steep g ⇒ density divided down."
