Markov chains · Steady-state behavior

A system hops among a few states over time. The only thing that governs where it goes next is where it is now — not how it got there. That is the Markov property. Collect the one-step move probabilities into a matrix P (each row sums to 1), and the whole future is just repeated multiplication: πₜ₊₁ = πₜ P. For a well-behaved chain the distribution πₜ forgets its start and settles onto a single stationary distribution π*. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

The Markov property — memoryless given the present

A Markov chain is the simplest interesting model of something that evolves in time. There is a finite set of states — here just three, A, B, C — and at each tick the process jumps from its current state to a next state at random. The defining assumption is a promise about memory: given that you are in state i right now, the probability you move to state j next depends only on i. The entire past — the route you took to get here — is irrelevant once I tell you the present. In symbols, P(Xₜ₊₁ = j | Xₜ = i, Xₜ₋₁, …, X₀) = P(Xₜ₊₁ = j | Xₜ = i) = Pᵢⱼ. That single sentence is the whole modeling commitment, and it is what makes everything downstream computable.

The transition matrix P

Stack the numbers Pᵢⱼ into a matrix. Row i lists where you go from state i, so every row is a probability distribution and must sum to 1 — that is the one rule P obeys (we call it row-stochastic). In the diagram each arrow i → j carries the weight Pᵢⱼ, and the self-loop is the "stay put" probability Pᵢᵢ. The sliders expose the two off-diagonal entries of each row; the diagonal is whatever is left over to make the row total 1, which is why you never have to touch it. The status box prints the resulting P so you can always read the chain you have built.

Distributions update by matrix multiplication

Suppose you are not sure where the chain starts: your belief is a row vector π₀ = (π₀[A], π₀[B], π₀[C]) of probabilities. Where are you likely to be one step later? To land in state j you must have been in some state i and then taken the i → j jump, so you sum over all the ways: π₁[j] = Σᵢ π₀[i] · Pᵢⱼ. That is exactly a vector-times-matrix product, π₁ = π₀ P. Do it again for two steps, π₂ = π₁ P = π₀ P², and in general πₜ = π₀ Pᵗ. The whole evolution is just hitting your belief vector with P over and over. Every time you nudge the t slider, the page performs one more multiplication and redraws the bars.

The stationary distribution π* and when you reach it

Watch the regular preset. From any start — A, B, C, or uniform — the bars march to the same final heights and then stop moving. That limit is the stationary distribution π*, the one belief that reproduces itself under a step: π* = π* P. Multiplying a row vector by P leaving it unchanged is precisely the statement that π* is a left eigenvector of P with eigenvalue 1 — the same eigenvalue-1 fixed direction you met in the linear-algebra Markov lecture. A row-stochastic matrix always has eigenvalue 1 (its rows sum to 1, so P times the all-ones column is the all-ones column); the content of the steady-state theorem is that for a regular chain — one where some power Pᵏ has all-positive entries, so every state can reach every other and the timing is not locked into a cycle — that eigenvalue-1 direction is unique and every start flows into it. The chain forgets where it began.

How fast it forgets is governed by the second-largest eigenvalue |λ₂|. The distance from stationarity shrinks like |λ₂|ᵗ: small |λ₂| means a brisk mixer, while |λ₂| close to 1 means a sluggish one. Press sticky — every state has a fat self-loop, so the chain dawdles in place and πₜ oozes toward π* over dozens of steps. The status box reports both the estimated |λ₂| and a mixing time: the first t at which the total-variation distance to π* drops below 1%.

When steady-state fails — periodicity and reducibility

Regularity is not free, and the two failure modes are worth seeing. Press periodic: it is the deterministic cycle A → B → C → A. A unique stationary distribution still exists — it is uniform (⅓, ⅓, ⅓), the long-run fraction of time spent in each state — but πₜ never settles onto it. Start in A and the probability mass marches around the triangle forever, one state at a time, a single tall bar that hops A → B → C → A and never spreads. The clock is locked: the chain returns to a state only at multiples of its period 3, so Pᵗ never becomes all-positive and the limit limₜ πₜ simply does not exist. Then press absorbing C: state C only points at itself, a one-way trap. Whatever happens early, the chain eventually wanders into C and is stuck, so π* = (0, 0, 1) regardless of start. Here the long-run is unique, but only because the other states are transient — change the trap and you can manufacture chains with several stationary distributions. Mixing-to-a-unique-limit needs both ingredients: you can reach everywhere (irreducible) and your timing is not periodic.

The link to PageRank and the eigenvalue-1 picture

This is not a toy. PageRank is exactly a stationary distribution: model a random surfer who, on each web page, follows an outgoing link at random (plus a small chance to teleport anywhere, which is the engineering trick that forces the chain to be regular so a unique π* exists). The importance of a page is its stationary probability — the long-run fraction of time the surfer spends there — and Google computes it by the same iteration you are dragging here: start anywhere, multiply by P repeatedly, and read off the eigenvalue-1 vector. Queueing systems, weather models, card shuffling, Markov chain Monte Carlo, and gene sequence models are all the same machine. Once you see that π* = π* P is just "find the eigenvalue-1 direction and normalize it to sum to 1," the linear algebra and the probability become one picture.
