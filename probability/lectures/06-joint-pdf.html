Lecture 6 · Joint PDFs and conditioning

Two continuous variables X and Y share a single surface, the joint density f(x,y). Probability is the volume sitting under that surface over a region. To condition on a measurement X = x₀ is to take a vertical slice through the joint and stretch it back up to area one — a renormalized cross-section. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

A density is a surface, and probability is volume

For a single continuous variable the density fX(x) is a curve, and probability is the area underneath it: P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫fX(x) dx. With two variables the density f(x,y) is a surface over the plane, and probability becomes volume: P((X,Y) ∈ B) = ∬B f(x,y) dx dy. The whole surface integrates to 1, just as the whole curve had area 1. The contour map on the left is that surface seen from above; each ring is a height level, and the volume over any region is "how much hill" sits above it.

Our running example is the standard bivariate normal, whose closed form is 

f(x,y) = 1 / (2π√(1−ρ²)) · exp( −(x² − 2ρxy + y²) / (2(1−ρ²)) )

Both marginals are standard normals; only the single number ρ (the correlation) ties them together. At ρ = 0 the contours are perfect circles. As |ρ| grows the circles stretch into tilted ellipses — positive ρ leans up the 45° line, negative ρ leans down it — and at the extremes the hill collapses onto a single ridge.

Marginals: flatten the hill onto one wall

To recover the density of Y alone, you no longer care about X, so you integrate it out: fY(y) = ∫ f(x,y) dx. Picture sweeping all the volume of the hill sideways and letting it pile up against the y-wall — the shadow it casts is the marginal. For our bivariate normal this collapsing always yields fY = N(0,1), no matter what ρ is. That is the faint grey bell on the right, and it is your baseline: it is what you'd believe about Y before you learn anything about X.

Conditioning = slice, then renormalize

Now suppose you measure X and it comes out x₀. The conditional density of Y is defined exactly as in the discrete case — joint over marginal: 

f(y | X = x₀) = f(x₀, y) / f_X(x₀)

Read it geometrically. The numerator f(x₀, y), as y varies, is the profile of the hill along the vertical line x = x₀ — the slice marked in white on the contour map. That profile has the right shape but the wrong area: it doesn't integrate to 1, because most of the hill's volume lives at other x values. Dividing by the constant fX(x₀) is precisely the rescaling that lifts the slice back up to total area 1. So conditioning is a slice followed by a renormalization, nothing more. The green curve on the right is that lifted slice.

For the standard bivariate normal the algebra is clean: complete the square in the exponent and the conditional comes out normal again, 

f(Y | X = x₀) = N( mean = ρ·x₀ ,  sd = √(1−ρ²) )

Two things move, and they are exactly what the picture shows. The mean shifts from 0 to ρx₀: learning that X is large drags your belief about Y in the correlated direction. And the variance shrinks from 1 to 1 − ρ²: a measurement always sharpens your knowledge, never blurs it. At ρ = 0.9 the conditional sd is √(1−0.81) ≈ 0.44 — less than half the original spread.

Independence is a factoring of the surface

Press the ρ = 0 preset. The contours become circles, and now slide x₀ back and forth: the green conditional does not move — it sits exactly on top of the grey marginal for every slice. That is the visual meaning of independence: knowing X tells you nothing about Y, so f(y | x₀) = fY(y) always. Equivalently, the joint surface factors, f(x,y) = fX(x) · fY(y) — the hill is a simple product of two bells with no tilt. For the bivariate normal this factoring happens if and only if ρ = 0. (That last "iff" is special to the normal; in general zero correlation does not force independence, but for jointly normal variables it does.)

The conditional mean is the regression line — the bridge to ISLR

Track the green curve's center as you drag x₀: it sits at E[Y | X = x₀] = ρx₀. Plot that center against x₀ and you trace the straight line y = ρx — the population regression of Y on X. This is not a coincidence of the normal; it is the deep link between probability and prediction. If you must guess Y from a measured X = x₀ and you are scored by mean-squared error, the optimal guess is the conditional expectation E[Y | X = x₀]. That single fact is the theoretical spine of An Introduction to Statistical Learning: regression is the business of estimating this conditional mean from data, and least squares works so cleanly for Gaussian-like data precisely because here the conditional mean is genuinely the straight line ρx. The slope ρ also explains regression to the mean: since |ρ| ≤ 1, the predicted Y is always closer to its mean than x₀ is to its own — extreme inputs forecast less-extreme outputs.
