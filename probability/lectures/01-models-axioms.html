Lecture 1 · Probability models and axioms

A probability model is two things: a sample space Ω — the set of all possible outcomes — and a probability law that assigns to each event A ⊆ Ω a number P(A) obeying just three axioms. Everything in the course is built on those three rules. Here Ω is the unit square [0,1]², an event is a region inside it, and P(A) is simply its area. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

What a probability model actually is

Tsitsiklis opens the course with a two-step recipe, and it never changes for the rest of the term. Step one: describe the sample space Ω, the set of all possible outcomes of the experiment — here, every point of the unit square. An event is any subset of Ω; A and B above are events. Step two: hand down a probability law that assigns a number P(A) to each event. The model is only as good as the law, and a law is only legal if it obeys the three axioms below. On this square the law is the simplest one imaginable — P(A) = the area of A — and you should check for yourself that it satisfies all three.

The three axioms

The entire edifice of probability rests on three rules. They are deliberately spare; everything else is a theorem.

  • (1) Nonnegativity. P(A) ≥ 0 for every event A. Areas are never negative, so this is automatic on the square. Probabilities are not allowed to be debts.
  • (2) Normalization. P(Ω) = 1. Something happens with certainty, so the whole sample space carries probability 1. The unit square has area exactly 1 — that is why we chose it.
  • (3) (Finite) additivity. If A and B are disjoint (A ∩ B = ∅), then P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B). Two non-overlapping regions have areas that simply add. (The full axiom is countable additivity — it holds for infinitely many disjoint events at once — but the finite version is all we need today.)

From these three lines you can derive every identity you will ever use: P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A), P(∅) = 0, monotonicity (A ⊆ B ⇒ P(A) ≤ P(B)), and the union bound. None of these are extra assumptions — they are consequences. That economy is the whole point of an axiomatic theory.

The intuition — probability is area

Why draw probabilities as areas? Because area already obeys all three axioms for free, so it is a perfect physical model of a probability law. An area is never negative (axiom 1); the whole square has area 1 (axiom 2); and two non-overlapping patches have areas that add (axiom 3). Throw a dart that is equally likely to land anywhere on the square — a uniform outcome — and the chance it lands in region A is exactly the area of A. Every dot in the picture is one such dart. The blue cloud's size is P(A); you are looking at probability directly. Keep this picture in your head: for the rest of 6.041, "probability of an event" and "how much of the sample space the event occupies" are the same sentence.

Why additivity needs disjointness

Axiom 3 has a hypothesis that students love to drop: the events must be disjoint. Press the disjoint preset — A and B don't touch, no purple appears, and the status box confirms P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) on the nose. Now drag B so it slides into A. The moment they overlap, the naive sum P(A) + P(B) overcounts: every purple dart got counted twice, once for being in A and once for being in B. Adding areas double-charges the shared patch. So P(A) + P(B) is no longer the area of the union — it is the area of the union plus the area of the overlap. Disjointness is exactly the condition that makes the overlap empty, and the double-count vanish.

Inclusion–exclusion — the fix for overlap

The repair is to subtract back the part you counted twice: 

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)
                              └──── the overlap, added once too often, removed once

This is the inclusion–exclusion formula, and it is not a new axiom — it follows from the three axioms by cutting the union into disjoint pieces and applying additivity to each. Write A ∪ B = (A only) ∪ (A ∩ B) ∪ (B only), three non-overlapping regions; then P(A) = (A only) + (A ∩ B) and P(B) = (B only) + (A ∩ B), and a line of algebra gives the formula above. Notice it gracefully contains axiom 3 as a special case: when A and B are disjoint, P(A ∩ B) = 0 and the correction term disappears. The status box computes both sides live, so you can watch P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B) stay locked to the true area of the union no matter how you drag the rectangles.

Independence is a different idea — don't confuse it with disjoint

Press the independent preset. Here A is a vertical strip (full height) and B is a horizontal strip (full width), so they cross in a small rectangle. Their areas multiply: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B) — that algebraic equation is the definition of independent events (knowing the dart is in the A-strip tells you nothing about whether it is in the B-strip). This is a trap worth flagging now, even though independence is a later lecture: disjoint and independent are opposites, not synonyms. Disjoint events can't both happen, so P(A ∩ B) = 0; if they also have positive probability, knowing A happened tells you B did not — the strongest possible dependence. Inclusion–exclusion holds for all events; multiplication P(A)P(B) is the special, fragile case of independence.

Worked numbers — the overlapping preset

Take the overlapping preset, where A is the rectangle [0.15, 0.60] × [0.20, 0.70] and B is [0.40, 0.85] × [0.35, 0.85]. Areas of axis-aligned rectangles are just width × height: 

P(A) = (0.60 − 0.15)·(0.70 − 0.20) = 0.45 · 0.50 = 0.225
P(B) = (0.85 − 0.40)·(0.85 − 0.35) = 0.45 · 0.50 = 0.225

A ∩ B  spans  x ∈ [0.40, 0.60],  y ∈ [0.35, 0.70]
P(A ∩ B) = (0.60 − 0.40)·(0.70 − 0.35) = 0.20 · 0.35 = 0.070

inclusion–exclusion:
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)
         = 0.225 + 0.225 − 0.070 = 0.380     ← NOT 0.45, which would double-count the overlap

The naive sum 0.225 + 0.225 = 0.450 is wrong by exactly 0.070 — the area of the purple intersection, counted twice and removed once. Read these four numbers off the status box and confirm the ≈ 1500 random dots reproduce them: roughly 0.38 × 1500 ≈ 570 dots should be colored (in A or B), with about 0.07 × 1500 ≈ 105 of them purple.

The frequentist meaning — area ≈ long-run fraction

The dots are not decoration; they are the bridge between the axioms and reality. Each press of ↻ new random outcomes reruns the experiment 1500 times. The law of large numbers (Lecture 19) promises that the fraction of darts landing in A converges to the area P(A) as the number of darts grows — probability is the long-run frequency of an event. With 1500 darts the empirical fractions in the status box already sit within a percent or two of the exact areas; resample a few times and watch them jitter around the true value rather than drift away from it. That convergence is why the area-model of probability predicts anything about the real world, and it is the experimental fact the rest of the course makes precise.
