Lecture 2 · Conditioning and Bayes' rule

Conditioning is just zooming in. Once you learn that event B happened, the world shrinks to B and probabilities are renormalized inside it: P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B). Bayes' rule reverses the arrow — it turns a test's P(+|disease) into the answer you actually want, P(disease|+). The famous trap is the base-rate fallacy: a 99%-accurate test for a rare disease is mostly wrong when it says "positive". ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)
The picture: the unit square on the left is the whole population. Its width is split by the true state — the thin green-ish strip is the diseased fraction P(D), the wide rest is healthy P(¬D). Its height inside each strip is split by the test result. So every one of the four rectangles is a joint probability, and its area is exactly that probability. The two positive rectangles are colored solid; everything else is faded. The stacked bar on the right collects only the positives and renormalizes them to height 1 — the green slice of that bar is the posterior P(D|+). Make the disease rare and watch the green slice shrink even when the test is excellent.

Conditioning shrinks the sample space

The definition P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B) looks like a formula but it is really a relabeling. Before you learn anything, the whole unit square has total probability 1. The moment you are told B occurred, you throw away the ¬B part of the square entirely and treat the leftover region B as your new universe. To keep probabilities adding to 1 you divide by how much of the original square B took up, P(B). Everything else — independence, Bayes' rule, Markov chains later in the course — is built on this one move. In the Bayes box above, "the test was positive" means: erase the two faded (negative) rectangles, keep the two solid columns of positive cases, and ask what fraction of that region is diseased.

Total probability: building the denominator

How likely is a positive test at all? A positive can arrive two ways — a sick person correctly flagged, or a healthy person wrongly flagged — so you add the two disjoint pieces. That is the total probability theorem: 

P(+) = P(+|D)·P(D)  +  P(+|¬D)·P(¬D)
        \_________/      \__________/
       true positives   false positives
        (TP, green)       (FP, orange)

Geometrically P(+) is the combined area of the two solid rectangles — exactly the total height of the right-hand "positives" bar before we renormalize it. The base rate P(D) weights each branch: when the disease is rare, the healthy branch P(+|¬D)·P(¬D) dominates the sum simply because there are so many more healthy people to mislabel.

Bayes' rule: reversing the conditioning

The test gives you P(+|D) — forward, from cause to symptom. You want P(D|+) — backward, from symptom to cause. Bayes' rule is the bridge, and it is just the definition of conditioning applied twice (numerator P(D∩+) written two ways) with total probability filling the denominator: 

            P(+|D)·P(D)                         TP
P(D|+) = ----------------------------- = ---------------
         P(+|D)·P(D) + P(+|¬D)·P(¬D)        TP + FP

Read it off the box: the posterior is the diseased (green) slice of the positive column. The prior P(D) is what you believed before the test; the posterior P(D|+) is what you should believe after. The test's job is to move you from one to the other — and how far it moves you depends on the prior you started from.

The base-rate fallacy — why screening a rare disease is hard

Press the rare disease (0.1%) + good test preset. The test catches 99% of the sick and false-alarms only 1% of the time — it sounds nearly flawless. Yet the posterior P(D|+) is only about 9%. The intuition the fallacy ignores: out of 100,000 people, only ~100 are sick (99 of them test positive), but ~99,900 are healthy, and 1% of those — about 999 people — also test positive. So the positives are 99 real against 999 false, and a positive result is still more likely to be a false alarm than a real case. 

P(D|+) = (0.99 · 0.001) / (0.99 · 0.001 + 0.01 · 0.999)
       = 0.00099 / (0.00099 + 0.00999)
       = 0.00099 / 0.01098  ≈  0.090   (about 9%)

The lesson is that accuracy alone is meaningless without the base rate. The same test on the common disease (30%) preset gives a posterior above 90% — identical test, opposite conclusion. This is exactly why doctors don't act on a single screening result for a rare condition; they confirm with a second, independent test. A second positive multiplies the odds again (Bayes applied a second time, with the first posterior as the new prior), and the green slice finally fills the bar.

The tie to ISLR and classification

Every probabilistic classifier in An Introduction to Statistical Learning is running this exact computation. A spam filter, a fraud model, a medical predictor — each combines the likelihood of the features given a class with the prior prevalence of that class to output a posterior, then thresholds it. The Bayes box is why a model trained on a balanced set degrades when deployed on a population where the positive class is rare: shifting the prior P(D) leftward, exactly as the slider does, collapses the precision (the share of predicted-positives that are truly positive) even though sensitivity and the false-positive rate never changed. Precision is P(D|+) — the green slice — and it lives or dies by the base rate.
