Lecture 5 · PDFs, CDFs, and the normal

A continuous random variable has no probability mass sitting on any single point — P(X = x) = 0 for every x. Probability lives in area. The density f(x) is a curve whose area over an interval is the chance of landing there: P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ab f(x) dx. The running area is the CDF F(x) = P(X ≤ x), and the two are tied by F′ = f. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

Density is not probability — area is

The single most common mistake with continuous variables is reading f(x) as "the probability of x." It isn't. For a continuous variable the probability of hitting any exact value is zero: P(X = x) = 0. There are uncountably many possible values, and you cannot give each a positive probability and still have them sum to 1. So what does the height of the curve mean? It is a density — probability per unit length. The density at a point can even exceed 1 (push the normal's σ below 0.4 and watch the peak climb past 1); only the area is a probability, and area is always between 0 and 1. The honest statement is the strip: P(x ≤ X ≤ x + dx) ≈ f(x) dx. Multiply the height by a width and you get a probability.

The probability of an interval is an integral

To get the chance of landing in a real interval, add up all those thin strips — that is exactly what the integral does: P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ab f(x) dx, the shaded region on the top plot. Two consequences fall straight out. First, the total area is 1: ∫−∞∞ f(x) dx = 1, because the variable has to land somewhere. Second, because a single point has zero width it has zero area, so the endpoints don't matter — P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = P(a < X < b). With a continuous variable you never have to fuss over ≤ versus <; that bookkeeping only mattered back when probability sat on isolated points.

The CDF: the running area, and it only goes up

Instead of carrying around an interval, fix the left end at −∞ and let the right end slide. That gives the cumulative distribution function F(x) = P(X ≤ x) — the total area under f from the far left up to x, drawn on the bottom plot. It always starts at F(−∞) = 0 (no area yet) and climbs to F(+∞) = 1 (all the area), and it can never decrease: sweeping rightward only ever adds non-negative density, so F is monotone. Its steepness is the density itself, F′(x) = f(x) — the CDF rises fastest exactly where the bell is tallest, and flattens out in the tails where there is almost nothing left to accumulate. The PDF and the CDF are not two distributions; they are one distribution differentiated and integrated.

Why the bracket equals the shaded area

The area from −∞ to b is F(b); the area from −∞ to a is F(a). Subtract the part you didn't want and the leftover is the strip in the middle: P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = F(b) − F(a). That is the fundamental theorem of calculus wearing a probability hat — the integral of f over [a, b] is the difference of the antiderivative F at the two ends. On the bottom plot the red bracket measures precisely that vertical gap F(b) − F(a), and the status box confirms it matches the shaded area on top to the last digit. This is why CDFs are the practical tool: areas under a curve are hard, but two table lookups and a subtraction are easy.

The normal, and the 68–95–99.7 rule

The normal (Gaussian) density f(x) = (1/(σ√(2π))) · e−(x−μ)²/(2σ²) is the bell you already met as the limit in the central limit theorem. Two numbers fix it: the center μ and the spread σ. Its CDF has no elementary closed form — there is no clean antiderivative of e−x² — so we evaluate it numerically (here via the error function, F(x) = ½[1 + erf((x−μ)/(σ√2))]). Because the shape never changes, the area within a fixed number of standard deviations is a universal constant, the empirical rule: about 68% within μ ± σ, 95% within μ ± 2σ, and 99.7% within μ ± 3σ. Press μ ± 1σ and μ ± 2σ above to set the bounds there and read those numbers straight off F(b) − F(a). Those three percentages are why "two-sigma" is shorthand for "surprising" and "three-sigma" for "almost never" — and why a 95% confidence interval is built from ± 1.96 σ, the bounds that fence off exactly 95% of this curve.

The exponential and the uniform — same machinery

Nothing above was special to the bell. The exponential(λ) models waiting times: density f(x) = λe−λx for x ≥ 0, with the clean closed-form CDF F(x) = 1 − e−λx. It is heavily front-loaded — most of the area sits near zero — and "memoryless." The uniform(ℓ, r) is the flat case: density 1/(r−ℓ) on the interval and 0 outside, so its CDF is a straight ramp (x−ℓ)/(r−ℓ) climbing from 0 to 1, and probability is just the fraction of the interval you cover. Switch between all three and watch the same story repeat: a density on top whose total area is 1, a monotone CDF on the bottom from 0 to 1, and the shaded interval always equal to the bracketed rise F(b) − F(a).
