Variance & joint PMFs

The mean tells you where a random variable sits; the variance tells you how far it typically strays. Var(X) = E[(X−μ)²] is the average squared distance from the mean, and its square root σ is the spread on the same scale as X. Then we put two variables in one table — a joint PMF p(x,y) — and watch what independence buys us: the variance of a sum just adds. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

What variance measures, and why we square

Fix the mean μ = E[X]. The deviation X − μ is how far a draw lands from the center — but it is sometimes positive, sometimes negative, and on average it is exactly zero (E[X − μ] = 0), so the raw deviation tells us nothing about spread. The fix is to square first: (X − μ)² is always ≥ 0, and its average is the variance:

Var(X) = E[(X − μ)²] = Σₖ (k − μ)² · p(k).

Read that as a weighted average of squared distances: each value k contributes its squared miss (k − μ)², weighted by how likely it is, p(k). Drag the slider above. At s = 0 every draw is the mean, so every term is zero and Var = 0 — a sure thing has no spread. Push mass outward and the far points, with the biggest (k−μ)², start to count: at the uniform setting Var = (4+1+0+1+4)/5 = 2, and at the U-shape, with all the mass sitting at ±2, Var = 4. Squaring is also why outliers dominate variance — a point twice as far away contributes four times as much.

The catch with squaring is units: if X is in dollars, Var(X) is in dollars², which means nothing to anyone. So we take the square root and define the standard deviation σ = √Var(X), back on the same scale as X. That is the half-width of the shaded band in the top plot. Tsitsiklis' mental model: σ is the typical distance of a draw from the mean — not exact, but the right order of magnitude, and the number you actually quote.

One algebraic shortcut worth memorizing, because it saves arithmetic every time: Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])². For our symmetric distribution μ = 0, so Var(X) = E[X²] exactly — which is why the variance is just the average of the squared values you see accumulating in the status box.

The joint PMF: one table for two variables

Now roll two fair dice and call the faces X and Y. A single number won't describe the pair; we need a whole table. The joint PMF p(x,y) = P(X = x and Y = y) assigns a probability to each of the 36 cells of the 6×6 grid in the heatmap, and — like any PMF — the entries are nonnegative and sum to 1: Σₓ Σᵧ p(x,y) = 1.

From the table you recover each variable alone by summing along a row or a column — these are the marginal PMFs: pₓ(x) = Σᵧ p(x,y) and pᵧ(y) = Σₓ p(x,y). For fair dice each margin is uniform, 1/6 on every face, in both the independent and the rigged settings. That is the whole point of the Y = X preset: the marginals do not pin down the joint table. Two very different worlds — fully independent versus perfectly locked together — have the identical margins. You cannot reconstruct the joint from the marginals; you need the table.

Independence is an assumption you can see

X and Y are independent when the joint factors into the product of the marginals, cell by cell: p(x,y) = pₓ(x) · pᵧ(y). For two fair dice that is (1/6)(1/6) = 1/36 in every cell — the flat heatmap of the independent preset. Knowing X tells you nothing about Y.

Flip to rigged Y = X. Now all the mass collapses onto the diagonal: p(k,k) = 1/6 and every off-diagonal cell is 0. The margins are still uniform 1/6 — but p(1,2) = 0 ≠ (1/6)(1/6), so the factorization fails and the variables are as dependent as can be: X determines Y exactly. Independence is not the default; it is a real structural claim about the table, and the heatmap lets you check it at a glance — uniform fill means independent, structure on the diagonal means dependent.

The derived distribution of the sum, and why variance adds

The sum S = X + Y is a function of the pair, so its PMF is a derived distribution: to get pₛ(s) we collect every cell of the joint table with x + y = s and add their probabilities. For independent dice that count runs 1,2,3,4,5,6,5,4,3,2,1 over s = 2…12, each over 36 — the familiar triangle, peaked at 7 because there are simply more ways to make a 7.

Expectation always adds, no assumptions needed: E[S] = E[X] + E[Y] = 3.5 + 3.5 = 7 by linearity, in both presets. Variance is the delicate one. The clean rule Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) holds only when X and Y are independent. Each fair die has Var = 35/12 ≈ 2.917, so for the independent dice Var(S) = 35/12 + 35/12 = 70/12 ≈ 5.833 — and you can confirm it directly from the triangular PMF. The general truth carries a correction term, Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) + 2·Cov(X,Y), and independence is exactly what makes the covariance vanish.

The rigged Y = X preset shows what happens when you forget the assumption. Now S = 2X, which lands only on the even values {2,4,6,8,10,12}, each with probability 1/6 — a totally different, much wider distribution. Scaling pulls the variance out by the square of the factor: Var(2X) = 4·Var(X) = 140/12 ≈ 11.667, exactly double the naive sum because here Cov(X,Y) = Var(X) is as large as it can be. Same margins, same E[S] = 7, but the spread of the sum is twice as big. That gap — 11.667 versus 5.833 — is the price of pretending dependent things are independent, and the status box keeps both numbers in front of you.
