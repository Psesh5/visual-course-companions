Limit theorems · The Central Limit Theorem

Take any distribution with a finite mean μ and variance σ². Average n independent draws. As n grows, the distribution of that average X̄ₙ becomes a normal curve centered at μ with standard deviation σ/√n — no matter what shape you started from. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

The smooth black curve is the normal N(μ, σ/√n) the theorem predicts. Drag n from 1 upward and watch the ragged population on the left collapse into that bell on the right.

What the theorem actually claims

Let X₁, X₂, …, Xₙ be independent draws from the same distribution, with mean μ = E[X] and variance σ² = Var(X). Form the average X̄ₙ = (X₁ + ⋯ + Xₙ) / n. Two things happen, and they are different claims:

  • Law of large numbers (the center). E[X̄ₙ] = μ exactly, for every n, and the spread SE = σ/√n shrinks to zero. So the average concentrates on the true mean. That is the “fixed population scale” view — flip to it and watch the histogram contract toward μ as you raise n.
  • Central limit theorem (the shape). The rescaled error (X̄ₙ − μ) / (σ/√n) converges to the standard normal, regardless of the original distribution. That is the “zoom to the mean” view — the bell fills the frame and matches the black curve even when the population is a die, a coin, or a skewed exponential.

The astonishing part is the universality: the limiting bell does not remember where it came from. Uniform, exponential, Bernoulli, a die, twin peaks — all of them flow to the same normal shape. Only two numbers survive the averaging, μ and σ; every other detail of the population is washed out.

The √n that runs all of statistics

The standard error SE = σ/√n is the most consequential formula in applied statistics. It says accuracy improves only with the square root of effort: to halve your error bars you need four times the data. Watch the status box — at n = 1 the spread is the full population σ; at n = 4 it has halved; at n = 25 it is a fifth. That single curve is why polls quote a margin of error, why √n appears in every confidence interval, and why “collect more data” has diminishing returns.

The bad case — when the CLT does not apply

Press the Cauchy preset and raise n. The histogram of averages refuses to settle — it stays wide and jumps around no matter how large n gets. The Cauchy distribution has such heavy tails that σ² is infinite; the average of n Cauchy draws is no better than a single draw. The CLT’s hypothesis — a finite variance — is not a footnote. This is the same warning that matters in finance: returns with fat tails break the normal approximation, and risk models that assume a bell quietly understate the odds of an extreme move.

Why this is the gateway to inference (and to ISLR)

Everything downstream is an application of the right-hand plot. A confidence interval is X̄ ± 1.96·σ/√n — the ±1.96 are the points that capture 95% of that normal curve. A hypothesis test asks how many SE’s the observed mean sits from a claim. The bootstrap in An Introduction to Statistical Learning resamples precisely to simulate this sampling distribution when the formula is out of reach. Once you can see the bell appear on the right, every standard error, p-value, and error bar in the rest of statistics is the same picture read off at different points.
