Covariance and correlation

Two random variables X and Y can move together. Covariance measures that co-movement, Cov(X,Y) = E[(X−μ_X)(Y−μ_Y)]; dividing by the two standard deviations rescales it into the correlation ρ ∈ [−1, 1], a unit-free score for the strength of the linear relationship. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

A point counts green when it sits in a same-sign quadrant (both above the means, or both below) — there the product (x−μ_X)(y−μ_Y) is positive. It counts red in an opposite-sign quadrant, where that product is negative. Covariance is just the average of those signed products: positive ρ floods the green diagonal, negative ρ the red one. What covariance measures

Center each variable on its own mean and look at the product of the two deviations, (X−μ_X)(Y−μ_Y). When X is above its mean and Y is above its mean, that product is positive; when both are below, the two negatives multiply to positive again. The product turns negative only when one variable is above its mean while the other is below. Covariance is the expected value of that product, Cov(X,Y) = E[(X−μ_X)(Y−μ_Y)], so its sign answers a single question: do the two variables tend to land in the same-sign quadrants (positive covariance, they rise and fall together) or in opposite-sign quadrants (negative covariance, one rises as the other falls)? Watch the green and red point counts in the status box as you sweep the slider — the average signed product is exactly what the cross-hair picture is showing you.

A useful identity, the analog of Var(X) = E[X²] − μ², is Cov(X,Y) = E[XY] − μ_X·μ_Y. And covariance generalizes variance: take Y = X and you get Cov(X,X) = E[(X−μ_X)²] = Var(X). That is why the diagonal of a covariance matrix holds the variances.

Standardizing: from covariance to correlation

Covariance has a flaw: it carries units. Measure heights in centimeters instead of meters and the covariance jumps by a factor of 100, even though the relationship is unchanged. The cure is to divide out both scales. Define the correlation coefficient 

        Cov(X,Y)          E[(X−μ_X)(Y−μ_Y)]
ρ  =  ───────────────  =  ─────────────────────
        σ_X · σ_Y            σ_X · σ_Y

Equivalently, ρ = E[X̃ Ỹ] where X̃ = (X−μ_X)/σ_X and Ỹ = (Y−μ_Y)/σ_Y are the standardized versions — each rescaled to mean 0 and variance 1. Standardizing strips away the units and the spread, leaving a pure, dimensionless number. That is why the slider above runs the same −1…1 range no matter what the variables actually represent.

Why ρ lives in [−1, 1] — Cauchy–Schwarz

The bound is not an accident; it is the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality in disguise. For any two zero-mean variables, E[X̃ Ỹ]² ≤ E[X̃²]·E[Ỹ²] = 1·1 = 1, so |ρ| ≤ 1. Think of random variables as vectors and the expectation E[X̃ Ỹ] as their inner product: correlation is literally the cosine of the angle between the centered variables. Cosines live in [−1, 1], and the extremes are reached only when the two vectors are parallel.

That gives the sharp geometric meaning of the endpoints: ρ = ±1 if and only if Y is an exact linear function of X, Y = aX + b with the sign of the slope a matching the sign of ρ. Drag the slider toward +1 and the cloud tightens onto a single rising line; toward −1 and it collapses onto a falling line. Anything in between is a fuzzier version of the same straight-line story — and "straight line" is the crucial fine print, as the next section shows.

The blind spot: uncorrelated is not independent

If X and Y are independent then Cov(X,Y) = 0, so independence forces ρ = 0. The converse is false, and the gap matters. Press the nonlinear U-shape preset: there we set Y = X² + tiny noise with X symmetric around 0. Now Y is almost completely determined by X — knowing X tells you nearly everything about Y — yet the sample correlation comes out near zero.

The reason is built into the definition. Because X is symmetric, every positive deviation (x−μ_X) has a mirror-image negative deviation that produces the same Y. The positive products on the right arm of the parabola are cancelled, term for term, by the negative products on the left arm, and the average signed product — the covariance — washes out to 0. Correlation only ever sees the linear trend; a symmetric bowl has no net linear trend even though it is a perfect, deterministic relationship. ρ = 0 means "no linear association," never "no association." That is the single most important caveat about correlation, and the U-shape is the picture to remember it by.

The tie to regression (ISLR) and multicollinearity

Correlation is the engine under simple linear regression. The least-squares line you see drawn in black has slope b = Cov(X,Y) / Var(X) = ρ · (σ_Y / σ_X): the regression slope is the correlation, rescaled by the ratio of spreads. When X and Y are standardized the slope is ρ, and the fraction of variance the line explains is R² = ρ². So a correlation of 0.9 already tells you the best line will capture about 0.81 of Y's variability — the slope you watch tilt above is correlation made visible.

In multiple regression the same quantity becomes a hazard. When two predictors are highly correlated — multicollinearity — the data cannot tell their separate effects apart, the coefficient estimates become wildly unstable, and their standard errors blow up. This is exactly the ρ → ±1 corner above: as the cloud degenerates onto a line, the two variables stop carrying independent information. An Introduction to Statistical Learning spends a whole section on detecting it, because a feature matrix whose columns are nearly collinear is the regression analog of a matrix that is nearly singular — and correlation is the number that tells you how close to that edge you stand.
