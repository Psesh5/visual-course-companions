Inequalities · The weak law of large numbers

Take any distribution with a finite mean μ and variance σ² and average n independent draws. The average X̄ₙ doesn't just tend toward μ — we can bound how far it strays, using nothing but those two numbers. Two simple inequalities (Markov, then Chebyshev) pin the average down, and the weak law of large numbers falls out in one line. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

On the right is the promise made by Chebyshev's inequality: the red curve 1/k² sits above the actual chance that X̄ₙ lands more than k standard errors from μ. The bound is always valid and usually loose — that gap is the price of asking nothing about the shape of the distribution.

Markov's inequality — the one-line ancestor

Everything here descends from a single, almost trivially simple fact about nonnegative random variables. If Y ≥ 0 and a > 0, then 

P(Y ≥ a)  ≤  E[Y] / a               ← Markov's inequality

The argument is one sentence, the way Tsitsiklis tells it: the mean E[Y] is an average of nonnegative values, and the slice of probability sitting out at Y ≥ a already contributes at least a times its own weight to that average. So that weight can be at most E[Y]/a — any more and the mean would overshoot. A worked number: if waiting times average E[Y] = 5 minutes, then P(Y ≥ 20) ≤ 5/20 = 0.25. We used only the mean and the fact that time can't be negative — nothing about the shape. That blindness is exactly why the bound is weak, and exactly why it always holds.

Chebyshev = Markov, aimed at the squared deviation

Now we want to control how far a variable X (mean μ, variance σ²) strays from its mean. The trick is to feed Markov a cleverly chosen nonnegative variable: the squared deviation (X − μ)², which is ≥ 0 and has mean exactly E[(X − μ)²] = σ². Apply Markov with a = ε²: 

P(|X − μ| ≥ ε)  =  P( (X − μ)² ≥ ε² )  ≤  E[(X−μ)²] / ε²  =  σ² / ε²
                                                          └── Chebyshev's inequality

That's the whole derivation: Chebyshev is just Markov applied to (X − μ)². Measure the distance in standard deviations by writing ε = kσ, and it collapses to the clean distribution-free statement P(|X − μ| ≥ kσ) ≤ 1/k². At k = 2: at most a quarter of the probability lies beyond two standard deviations, for any distribution with a finite variance. The red curve on the right is precisely this 1/k², and it sits above the true tail every time.

The weak law — a one-line corollary

Apply Chebyshev not to a single draw but to the sample average X̄ₙ = (X₁ + ⋯ + Xₙ)/n. Its mean is still μ, but its variance is Var(X̄ₙ) = σ²/n — the averaging shrinks the spread. Plug that into Chebyshev: 

P(|X̄ₙ − μ| ≥ ε)  ≤  Var(X̄ₙ) / ε²  =  σ² / (n ε²)   ──→  0   as n → ∞

Fix any tolerance ε, however small. The right-hand side has an n in the denominator, so it marches to zero. That is the weak law of large numbers: for every fixed ε > 0, the probability that the sample average misses μ by more than ε goes to 0 as the sample grows. The left-hand plot is this statement in motion — slide the band tighter with the ε control and the runs still find their way inside and stay. The status box shows σ²/(nε²) for the current numbers and how shrinking it is the weak law itself.

"The average concentrates" vs. "the shape becomes normal"

It's worth being precise about what this lecture does and does not say, because the Central Limit Theorem next door is often blurred together with it. The weak law is a statement about concentration: the average collapses onto the single point μ, and the spread σ/√n goes to zero. That is the funnel you see on the left. The CLT is a sharper, different statement about shape: if you zoom in and rescale the leftover error by exactly σ/√n, what remains is always the same bell curve, no matter what population you started from. The weak law tells you the dart lands near the bullseye; the CLT tells you the precise Gaussian pattern of the near-misses. This page is the natural step right before that one — open the CLT lecture to watch the rescaled error converge to the normal.

Why settle for a loose bound?

Drag n up and read the gap between the red Chebyshev curve and the actual tail: at k = 2 Chebyshev promises ≤ 0.25, while the true chance (for a near-normal X̄ₙ) is only about 0.046 — Chebyshev is loose by more than a factor of five. So why use it? Because it is assumption-light. The CLT's tighter 2(1 − Φ(k)) estimate (the dashed curve) needs n to be large enough for the bell to set in; Chebyshev needs only a finite variance and holds for every n, every distribution, exactly. When you know nothing but μ and σ² — no shape, no tails, no large-n license — a guaranteed-valid bound beats a sharp guess. That trade, robustness for tightness, is the whole personality of these inequalities, and it is why the weak law can be proved in a single honest line.
