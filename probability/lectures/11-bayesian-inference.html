Bayesian inference · MAP and LMS estimates

A coin has an unknown bias θ ∈ [0,1] — the probability of heads. We start with a prior belief θ ~ Beta(a, b), then flip the coin. Each flip nudges the belief. After h heads and t tails the posterior is exactly Beta(a+h, b+t): the curve sharpens and slides toward the true value. Bayesian inference turns data into an updated distribution over the unknown, not just a single number. ▶ watch on OCW (Tsitsiklis 6.041)

Bayes' rule is the whole engine: p(θ | data) ∝ p(θ) · p(data | θ). The unknown here is the continuous bias θ, so the prior p(θ) is a density on [0,1]. If we model flips as independent Bernoulli(θ) trials, then h heads and t tails have likelihood θh(1−θ)t. Multiply, and the normalizing constant p(data) is just whatever makes the result integrate to 1 — we never need to compute it to find the shape. The posterior is proportional to p(θ) · θh(1−θ)t, and its shape is everything: it is the answer.

Why Beta stays Beta — conjugacy

A Beta(a, b) density is itself proportional to θa−1(1−θ)b−1. Watch what the multiplication does: 

posterior  ∝  prior            ×  likelihood
           ∝  θ^(a−1)(1−θ)^(b−1) ×  θ^h (1−θ)^t
           =  θ^(a+h−1) (1−θ)^(b+t−1)        ← exactly a Beta(a+h, b+t) kernel

The exponents simply add. So the posterior is again a Beta, with updated counts a → a+h and b → b+t. The Beta family is conjugate to the Bernoulli likelihood: data never knocks you out of the family, it just bumps the two parameters. This is why Bayesian updating here is bookkeeping — you can read a as “prior heads” and b as “prior tails,” pseudo-observations you walked in with. A flat Beta(1,1) is one imaginary head and one imaginary tail; the skeptical Beta(0.5,0.5) is half of each.

MAP vs LMS — the peak vs the center of mass

A posterior is a full distribution, but often you must report one number. Two principled choices:

  • MAP (maximum a posteriori). The mode — the single most probable θ, where the posterior peaks. For Beta(α, β) with α, β > 1 that is θ̂MAP = (α−1)/(α+β−2) = (a+h−1)/(a+b+h+t−2).
  • LMS (least mean squares) = posterior mean. The center of mass, θ̂LMS = E[θ | data] = α/(α+β) = (a+h)/(a+b+h+t). It is the estimate that minimizes the expected squared error E[(θ−θ̂)²|data] — which is why it is the default for a quadratic loss.

Worked example: start flat, Beta(1,1), and observe 8 heads in 10 flips. Posterior Beta(9, 3). The MAP is (9−1)/(12−2) = 8/10 = 0.80 — exactly the sample frequency h/N, because with a flat prior the MAP is the maximum-likelihood estimate. The LMS mean is 9/12 = 0.75, pulled a hair toward ½ by that one imaginary head and tail. For a skewed posterior the mode and the mean genuinely disagree; the longer tail drags the mean away from the peak. As data accumulates the posterior turns symmetric and tight, and the two estimates converge — drag the prior around or flip a thousand times and watch θ̂MAP and θ̂LMS in the status box collapse onto each other and onto the true θ.

The prior washes out

Both estimates are weighted blends of the prior and the data. Write the LMS mean as (a+h)/(a+b+N): for small N the prior counts a, b dominate, so a strong Beta(20,20) keeps you near 0.5 no matter what the first few flips say. But the denominator grows with every flip, so for large N the estimate ≈ h/N — the prior's fixed contribution is drowned by the mounting evidence. The posterior standard deviation √(αβ / [(α+β)²(α+β+1)]) shrinks like 1/√N, the same square-root law from the CLT. Two people who started with different priors but see the same long data stream end up with nearly identical posteriors. This is the Bayesian echo of consistency: enough data, and your starting assumptions stop mattering. (The exception: a prior that assigns zero density to the truth — e.g. swearing θ = 0 — can never recover. Don't put probability 0 on anything you might have to believe.)

The bridge to ISLR — a prior is a penalty

Take logs of posterior ∝ prior × likelihood: log p(θ|data) = log p(data|θ) + log p(θ) + const. Maximizing the posterior (finding the MAP) is therefore maximizing the log-likelihood plus a term from the prior — and that extra term is exactly a regularization penalty. In An Introduction to Statistical Learning, ridge regression adds λ‖β‖² to the least-squares loss; that is the MAP estimate under a Gaussian prior on the coefficients (a Gaussian's log is a negative square). Lasso's λ‖β‖₁ penalty is the MAP under a Laplace prior. The penalty strength λ is just how confident the prior is — a tight prior (large λ) is our strong Beta(20,20) that resists the data; a vague prior (small λ) is the flat Beta(1,1) that lets the data speak. “Regularize to avoid overfitting” and “use a prior to avoid overreacting to small samples” are the same sentence in two dialects: the prior keeps your estimate sane when the data is thin, and politely steps aside once the data is rich.
